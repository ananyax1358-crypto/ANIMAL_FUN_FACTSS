GREENLAND SHARKS

Greenland sharks may live 250–400 years — and it changes how their mind works Greenland sharks grow and age so slowly that their brains develop over centuries. Psychology implication: their perception of time, learning, and stress is radically different from fast-lived animals. A threat or stimulus that feels “urgent” to humans may register as almost insignificant to them. Researchers believe this extreme longevity likely comes with:
Very slow emotional reactivity

Minimal stress response

Long-term memory without urgency

AXOLOTLS

Axolotls don’t just regenerate limbs — they may not experience trauma the same way Axolotls can regenerate their brain tissue, spinal cord, and even parts of the heart without forming scar tissue. Psychology / neuroscience angle: In humans, trauma is partly “locked in” by neural scarring and rigid memory pathways. Axolotls, however, can rebuild neural circuits almost perfectly, suggesting: Minimal long-term fear conditioning No permanent trauma encoding A nervous system biased toward reset rather than rumination This makes them a major model organism for studying: PTSD prevention Neural plasticity Memory rewriting
